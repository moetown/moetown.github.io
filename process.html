01.

Discovery

The goal of untangling customer problems early in the discovery phase is to get insight into the width and depth of the problem. It is crucial to empathise with the customer, and figure out what their needs are.


Methods:

Workshops, surveys, semi-structured interviews, focus groups, data aggregation, insight analysis


Tools:

Whiteboard, Post-its, FigJam, Looker Studio reports, Google Analytics




Problem to solve

A stated customer problem might derive from different underlying needs, which in themselves, might mean different problems to solve. A hypothetical example: A large number of users are requesting a way to print out a reciept after a transaction. However, the users might come from different user segments - one needing it for their business, one needing it for their own records. Since the users represent two different segments, it might be good to validate weather there's multiple underlying needs which are not met due to this nature.

Running workshops, interviews, observation studies, and listen-ins are a crucial part in that process.

Our users

Synthesising user insights into user journeys, personas/archetypes and other segmented visuals is a way to present various user needs to stakeholders involved in the disvery process.

Having the stakeholder team involved in these steps and engaged in the research is key in my opinion :)

02.

Ideation

Next step would be to work through the research and bring it into hypothetical ideas for how we might help the customer achive what they're trying to do. I typically run workshops in FigJam or with post-its. These days I've found that the Oportunity Solution Tree has been helpful in reaching a shared understanding of how everything is connected.



Methods:

Brainstorming, dot-voting, oportunity solution tree


Tools:

FigJam, post-its, whiteboard



Sketching

Once the ideation has been carried out, what I'd typically do is start sketching out wireframes for how the idea might work. If we're doing a Design Spint, everyone in the group participates.

When I have a rough visual, I'll share it with the team who was part of the ideation for initial feedback. I might also verify it with the target users, depending on the size of the project.

Fine tuning

As the experience is refined, the design will start to get more and more production worthy. The way I see it is that the concept should get more and more "pixel perfect" with every feedback loop, keeping people involved and engaged throughout the journey. For a small scope this might be done within a day, for a large intiative this might take weeks.

A rule of thumb is that design is never done, as customer problems are endless and ever changing. At some point though the design will be "good enough for now, safe enough to try". Demoing the progress to everyone with stake will help determine when this point has been reached.

03.

Testing

You will want feedback on your design from your users. They're the ones who are going to use your product. It goes without saying that it's more expensive to change the design after you've built it in code, than in a prototype. Save time and money by getting the answers early :)




Methods:

Concept testing, Usability testing, Surveys, Internal presentations


Tools:

Figma, Useberry, Looker Studio, Invision



Concept & usability test

Every initiative has a different scope, but a rule of thumb is to test at least with 5-10 users per iteration you're doing. This can heavily depend on a lot of factors: How easy access do you have to your customers for feedback? How intrerested are they in the problem you're trying to solve - are they target users? Are there any incentives? How important is the feedback to the scope?. I typically prepare the tests with a set of research questions, which I turn into interview quiestions. These are based on what we aim to validate or invalidate throughout each session. Depending on the outcome of the tests, I might tweak the design for another iteration.

04.

Development & Design

The product team divides the scope of development with story mapping, ensuring we build it with the most essential functionality first - test internally - and then continue. In this way, we can learn as we go. As we get to production, we aim to release a first version which we can get feedback from and continue to iterate on.



Methods:

Story mapping, Design System, Design Guidelines, Iteration plan.


Tools:

Figma, Jira, Test-server, Feedback & data tools



Design delivery

I typically present the design in scenario flows in Figma, allowing the developers to follow the customer through a happy path, error path etc. There might be a need for a protoype, and some new guidelines in our design system if we build new components.

Working closely with developers and testers is in this part of the project so much fun, and you deliver the end solution together as a team. Following up on the release through closely monitoring KPIs, customer feedback and interactions in GA helps us determine whether we've successfully solved the problem in a desirable way.
