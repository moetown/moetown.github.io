Project start

The initial roadmap is crucial for our product's success

Every new project has variables. Timeframes, requests, visual requirements, stakeholders, style and much more. I try to keep it simple and just listen and ask questions initially.

My job is to make your vision come alive and at the same time combine that with the user's ultimate experience. It involves a lot of communication and collaboration, ideation, and quick problem solving. As a multi-tasker, this is perhaps one of my more refined skills: keeping a lot of things going at once.

Early ideation

some examples of tools and methods

The teams I've been working with previously have typically mapped out ideas on a whiteboard in all phases of the project. There are so many benefits to this, as all involved can be part of the creation and give their input as to what's going to happen. Early architecture, wireframes, timelines - all can be easily created and modified. If not a whiteboard, we'd typically use big A1 papers to do the same exercice. This can also be turned into post-it brainstorming sessions, De Bonos thinking hats, KJ analysis, etc.

The next step would be to test out the hypotheses of what our design should be. So this is where running workshops, interviews, observation studies, and listen-ins become crucial. Examples from previous work can be found in the section about my thesis work with Ericsson & Semcon.

01.

Brief

Defining the project from a requirement standpoint - Mapping it out in Trello, Asana, or a Gantt chart. Target user group defined. User needs are highlighted and prioritized.

02.

Hypothesis

Brainstorm with architectural approach, sketches, and whiteboard wireframes.

Analytics and Hotjar for guidance. Personas created, storyboards built, and requirements reviewed.

03.

User validation

Paper mockups and/or Sketch mockups with A/B testing. Analysing data through observation and/or heatmaps. Surveys before and after the test for qualitative data. Workshops and focus groups.

Ideation, iteration & creation

The next step in the process is to work with the requirements and findings from the pre-study. I typically would make paper mockups and run focus groups or simple proof of concepts in Sketch, and then test them using Invision. A/B testing, observation studies, and follow-up interviews would be my ideal approach.

Testing

Trying out the concept ideas

When reaching the final concept, it should ideally be quite defined what the product is and how it's going to look. Testing is then conducted to adjust smaller semantic impressions, animations, and other interaction-specific elements with A/B versions of the investigated look and feel.
